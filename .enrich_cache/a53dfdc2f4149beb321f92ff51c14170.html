Bridging Gaps in Crisis Communication: Strengthening Disaster Preparedness in Nepal

Nepal is one of the most disaster-prone countries in the world, encountering series of threats and hazards each year. Every year, communities, infrastructure and livelihoods are exposed to risks from monsoon floods, landslides, glacier lake outburst floods (GLOFs), earthquakes as well as winter hazards such as snowstorms, avalanches and extreme cold waves. Climate change is further intensifying these threats by causing erratic rainfall, accelerating glacier melt, altering snowfall patterns and making weather conditions increasingly unpredictable across seasons. As a result, the frequency and severity of disasters are rising. While the country has made notable strides in disaster risk reduction, one persistent challenge remains: effective crisis communication. In a multi-hazard scenario, where different threats may occur simultaneously or sequentially, clear, timely and trusted communication becomes even more critical.

Recent events have made the country’s gaps in disaster management and lack of effective crisis communication more prominent than ever. In November 2025, an avalanche on Mount YalungRi in Dolakha District killed seven people including five foreign climbers, two Nepali guides and injured others, while rescue efforts were severely hamperedby poor visibility and deep snow. Earlier, in October 2025, heavy snowfall disrupted trekking routes in Upper Mustang, Manang and the Annapurna region, temporarily stranding trekkers and blocking roads.These incidents, along with reports of heavy snow accumulation in high-altitude regions highlight Nepal’s vulnerability to sudden and severe winter hazards. They also expose country’s weaknesses in crisis communication and disaster response systems. Without reliable early warning systems, adequate infrastructure and clear communication strategies, timely and effective response to such events remains a major challenge.

Identified Gaps and Possible Solutions

First of all, automation is an area where Nepal’s crisis communication system remains underdeveloped. Warnings are often created and shared manually after situations are checked and confirmed, which slows down the process at a time when speed is critical. In many advanced systems, alerts are automatically issued when danger thresholds are reached, such as rising river levels or heavy rainfall triggering landslides.Nepal still lacks systems that can generate automated alerts based on predefined thresholds or effectively use risk prediction models. Tools like rainfall forecasting and landslide mapping are also not fully integrated with real-time public warning systems.

Nepal’s early warning systems remain limited in scope due to several underlying systemic challenges. One of the major issues is the difficulty in reaching and effectively communicating with vulnerable communities. Early warnings are only useful if people can understand and act on them, yet many remote villages face connectivity barriers and limited awareness of disaster response. Gaps in last-mile communication, combined with low levels of community trust, continue to undermine the effectiveness of existing systems.Further, inclusion is a critical but often overlooked dimension of the communication gap. Nepal’s linguistic, geographic and socio-economic diversity means that a “one-message-fits-all” system is fundamentally inadequate. With more than 120 languages spoken, many official alerts issued only in Nepali exclude large segments of the population. This is not just a linguistic issue but a technical design flaw in alert systems, which rarely support automated multilingual output. A more inclusive system would embed language processing engines capable of generating alerts in multiple local languages such as Maithili, Bhojpuri, Tamang and Tharu in real time. Without this, early warning systems risk becoming technically efficient but socially ineffective.

Digital exclusion further compounds the problem. Although mobile penetration is high, smartphone access and digital literacy remain uneven, particularly among elderly population, women in rural areas and economically marginalized groups. Crisis communication systems that rely heavily on smartphone applications or internet-based platforms inadvertently exclude these groups. Even social media platforms, while useful for rapid dissemination, primarily reach digitally active population and can amplify misinformation if not properly managed. Rumors during disasters can spread quickly, undermining official guidance. Further, short lead times for predictions reduce the opportunities for effective preparedness or evacuation. Inclusion requires a hybrid communication system that combines advanced technology with simpler, easy-to-access methods such as FM radio, community notice boards and trained local volunteers.

Another key challenge is the lack of proper community engagement, which prevents crisis communication from functioning smoothly. Effective disaster management should ensure that all members of the community, especially women and girls are actively involved in both decision-making and response efforts. While women and girls are often seen as the most vulnerable during disasters, they can play important roles as leaders and agents of change. Their participation is therefore essential for strengthening both disaster response and crisis communication systems. Engaging women’s groups such as cooperatives, mothers’ groups (AamaSamuha) and female community health volunteers can strengthen communication networks. These groups are trusted sources of information and when early warnings reach them, the information spreads quickly throughout the community. Likewise, local youth clubs play an increasingly vital role in disseminating weather alerts, translating warnings for children and elderly people and assisting in evacuations. With higher digital literacy compared to other age groups, young people serve as effective communicators between formal early warning systems and community-level action. Involving youth in these efforts helps bridge the gap between official alerts and local responses, making disaster preparedness and response more effective.

Lastly but most importantly, there is a lack of adequate capacity across all levels of the government in Nepal: national, provincial and municipal. Key gaps include insufficient training, unclear delegation of responsibilities and the absence of standardized protocols. Strengthening institutional capacity, clearly defining roles and establishing consistent procedures are essential to improving effectiveness at each level. Enhancing institutional capacity involves training personnel to accurately interpret forecasts, issue timely alerts and communications and effectively coordinate response efforts.Clear responsibilities are necessary to avoid confusion or delays during emergencies, ensuring that every agency, department and local authority knows exactly what actions to take, when and how. Standardized protocols allow different institutions to communicate seamlessly, share data and activate response mechanisms efficiently, reducing overlaps or gaps. Without these elements, even technically advanced early warning systems may fail to reach vulnerable communities, leading to delays in evacuation, resource mobilization and life-saving interventions. Strong institutional frameworks also enable long-term sustainability, ensuring that systems remain functional, updated and responsive, despite changes in leadership or disaster scenarios in the future.

Looking Ahead

Nepal’s growing exposure to climate-induced hazards makes it clear that strengthening crisis communication is no longer optional but essential. A resilient and advance early warning ecosystem is needed not only to deliver timely alerts before disasters escalate, but also to close persistent communication gaps that continue to put lives at risk.Nepal must move beyond reactive responses and adopt a proactive approach in which communication is continuous, built on awareness, education and trust. Effective crisis communication begins long before a disaster occurs and depends on how well communities understand risks and how confidently they respond to warnings.

Ultimately, improving Nepal’s system requires a balanced approach that combines technological advancement, proper governance coordination and strong community inclusion. A system that is highly developed but lacks clear coordination among government agencies or fails to reach marginalized groups will be ineffective, just as an inclusive but poorly coordinated system will lack reliability. By investing in an advance, well-integrated and inclusive communication system, Nepal can transform its vulnerability into preparedness, ensuring that timely and accurate information reaches all communities and no one is left uninformed when disaster strikes.
