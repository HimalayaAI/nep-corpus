The Missing Foundation for Nepal’s Digital Ambitions

The Missing Foundation for Nepal’s Digital Ambitions

Global momentum around Nepal’s digital transformation is accelerating. Last month’sannouncement of the World Bank and Asian Development Bank’s joint project total of USD 90 million, investment in Nepal’s digital public infrastructure (DPI) marks the largest to date in the country. DPI - foundational digital systems that enable secure, seamless interactions between people, businesses, and governments -is increasingly seen as the backbone of a modern state.

But infrastructure alone does not guarantee transformation. Nepal now faces a pivotal choice: whether to build a digital ecosystem that is truly integrated, inclusive, and trusted – or one that simply digitizes existing fragmentation.

At the heart of this choice lies a less visible, but highly consequential system: civil registration and vital statistics (CRVS).

CRVS refers to the continuous, universal, and timely recording of births, deaths, marriages, divorces and causes of death in the population. It is not just a technical bureaucratic system; it is the foundation of a functioning state. When CRVS serves as the foundation for DPI, it enables governments to know – accurately and in real time – who exists, who needs services, and who is eligible to receive them.

A Registry That Cannot See Itself

Nepal’s current digital ecosystem falls short of this foundation. While the country collects more data than ever before, the weakest link is civil registration itself and the systems remain fragmented and poorly operational. Birth registration remains incomplete, particularly in remote districts; death registration is weaker still, with fewer than half of all deaths formally recorded and cause-of-death data rarely captured in usable form. These CRVS gaps flow directly into every layer above them.

The Nagarik App currently allows citizens to view information, but most services cannot yet be completed end-to-end. The National ID system legal framework is constrained by outdated legal frameworks, limited to citizens over 16 and requiring physical smartcards design choices rooted in a pre-digital era. And Nepal still lacks a national data exchange framework to enable secure, seamless sharing of information across ministries.

The result is not transformation, but digitized fragmentation.

This has real consequences. Nepal spendsover NPR 134 billion annually over more than USD one billion on Social Security Allowances for elderly, widows, people with disabilities, children and marginalized groups. These programs depend fundamentally on accurate, up-to-date population data. Without real-time integration between civil registration and social protection systems, payments continue to flow to individuals who have died or are no longer eligible.

These are not minor inefficiencies. They are structural failures that undermine trust, waste public resources, and limit the effectiveness of government programs. Building digital services on top of disconnected registries is like constructing roads that do not meet.

Encoded Exclusion

The risk extends beyond efficiency. If not designed carefully, digital systems can deepen existing inequalities. The communities most affected by digital transformation—women, rural households, indigenous groups, people with disabilities, migrants—are often the least represented in system design.

Recent data illustrates this gap. While mobile phone ownership is at gender parity among men and women, usage remains staggeringly unequal. Only 5 percent of women in Nepal hold a mobile money account, compared to 17 percent of men. And only 3 percent of women have used a phone or the internet to access a bank account. But the exclusion begins earlier, in the civil registry.When a birth goes unregistered as still happens disproportionately for girls in rural and remote communities. Without deliberate design choices that begin with CRVS, digital systems will not close these disparities. They will encode them.

The Governance Dividend

Done right, however, integrating CRVS and DPI has the potential to fundamentally strengthen governance in Nepal. A robust CRVS system would allow the National Statistics Office to produce accurate, register-based population estimates between censuses - eliminating the chronic ‘denominator problem' that distorts everything from estimates of vaccination coverage to school enrollment data. The Ministry of Health could track mortality trends in real time, rather than relying on data that is years out of date. Provinces like Karnali, where death registration completeness remains low despite highest development need, could receive targeted investment based on evidence, not assumption.

Even budget execution could improve. Provincial governments underspent their budgets by 34 percent in 2023, partly due to weak data for planning and allocation. Reliable, integrated population data would enable more precise, evidence-based local decision-making at every level of government.

The issue is not whether Nepal should invest in digital systems – it is how those systems are designed.

CRVS must become the backbone of an authoritative population register that anchors the entire ecosystem. A birth recorded in a health facility should automatically trigger registration, assignment of a national ID, and enrolment in relevant social programmes, without requiring a parent to navigate multiple bureaucratic processes. Systems like the VERSP-MIS developed with World Bank, UNICEF, and WHO support already provide a foundation for this kind of integration, linking Department of National ID ( DoNIDCR) with the National Statistical Office and social protection systems. Nepal should be building on these efforts, not bypassing them.

Equally important are the enabling conditions of trust. Electronic signatures and Public Key Infrastructure are not optional features – they are the prerequisites for secure digital transactions. Nepal should treat CRVS as the authoritative population backbone. A registered birth should trigger identity, service eligibility, and programme enrolment; a registered death should automatically update the records that determine benefits and services. That is what digital public infrastructure is supposed to do.

The Foundation Is Still Within Reach

Civil society groups have already raised concerns about fragmented digital governance and weak donor coordination across Nepal’s digital landscape. These concerns are well-founded. Across the developing world, digital government initiatives have too often produced systems without delivering outcomes because they treated technology as the solution, rather than comprehensive governance design.

Nepal has the opportunity to avoid that mistake.The country has political momentum, institutional capacity, and unprecedented financing aligned around its digital transformation. But without a strong foundation in civil registration and vital statistics, these investments risk falling short of their promise. Digital transformation is not about apps or platforms. It is about building systems that work- consistently, inclusively, and at scale. For Nepal, that work must begin with CRVS.

Nishant Khanal is an economist.

Siddhi Aryal Ph D is Asia regional director at Vital Strategies.
